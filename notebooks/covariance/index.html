The geometric interpretation of the covariance matrix¶

Covariance matrices¶

A multivariate dataset will typically be a matrix whose rows are observations and whose columns are features of those observations (or the other way around, but we will stick to the former convention here). The variables may have different variances, and they may covary to a smaller or larger degree. All that information is contained in the covariance matrix.

data = data.drop(columns=["age", "male"])
# tranpose the matrix to the format used in maths books
data = data.transpose()

#plt.ylim(140, 180)
#plt.xlim(25, 65)
plt.scatter(data.loc[["weight"],:], data.loc[["height"],:])
plt.gca().set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')

We see a clear covariance between height and weight. The mathematical definition of covariance between two variables $x$ and $y$ is

$$\frac{1}{n-1}\displaystyle\sum_1^{n} (x_n - \bar{x})(y_n - \bar{y}) $$

where $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$ are the means of vectors $x$ and $y$. So if both $x_n$ and $y_n$ are above or below their mean, the product is positive. Similarly, the variance of vector $x$ is just its covariance with itself:

$$\frac{1}{n-1}\displaystyle\sum_1^{n} (x_n - \bar{x})^2$$

Since we subtract the mean when computing the covariance, it is useful to center our data by substracting the mean from each observation.

And now, since we have centered the data, the covariance between observation vectors $x$ and $y$ is just the dot product $x \cdot y$ and the variance of $x$ is just $x \cdot x$. And to get the covariance matrix, i.e. the dot products of each row with all the other rows, we just multiply the transpose of our data matrix with itself

$$\frac{1}{n-1}DD^T$$

Geometric interpretation of the covariance matrix¶

The covariance matrix of a dataset is a linear transformation that transforms white data (i.e. random, normal distributed data) into the observed data!

Let us look at some white data

The actual covariance matrix of the random normal data will deviate a little from the theoretical covariance matrix, which is just the identity matrix:

$$D_nD_n^T = I$$

Now we want to find the linear transformation $T$ that transforms white data $D_n$ into the observed data $D$. $T$ can be decomposed into a rotation matrix $R$ and a diagonal scaling matrix $M$.

$$D = TD_n = RMD_n$$

Now the covariance matrix of the observed data is $DD^T$.

$$ S = DD^T = (RMD_n)(RMD_n)^T = (RMD_n)((RM)(D_n))^T = (RMD_n)D_n^T(RM)^T = (RM)(D_nD_n^T)(RM)^T = (RM)(RM)^T = RMM^TR^T = RMMR^T $$

because $M$ is a diagonal matrix identical to its own transpose. So if we can decompose the covariance matrix into matrices $R$ and $M$ such that $S = RMMR^T$, we have the linear transformation $T = RM$ that turns white data into the observed data. And it turns out that the eigenvalue decomposition of $S = V\Lambda V^{-1} = V\Lambda^{\frac{1}{2}}\Lambda^{\frac{1}{2}}V^{-1}$ gives us exactly that, where $\Lambda$ is a matrix with the eigenvalues of $S$ on its diagonal and $V$ has the corresponding eigenvectors in the same order. (See the notebook on MDS for more on the eigenvalue decomposition.)

Eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix¶

Lets look at the covariance matrix in another way. The scalar projection $a_b$ of a vector $a$ onto a vector $b$ is given by

$$a_b = a \cdot \frac{b}{||b||} $$

i.e. the dot product of $a$ and the unit vector in the direction of $b$. Now if our data are columns of $D$ we can project them all onto some vector unit vector $a$ with

$$a^TM$$

For example, we can project our centered data onto $ a_1 = (0, 1)^T$, which will give us only the weight axis, or onto $a_2 = (\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^T$, which will give us an axis that "pays equal attention" to height and weight.

Now the question we ask ourselves is: how can we choose the vector $a$ so as to maximize the variance of the projected data $v^TD$. Recall that the variance of the projected data is given by

$$(v^TD)(v^TD)^T = (v^TD)(D^Tv) = v^T(DD^T)v = v^TSv$$

where $S$ is the covariance matrix of the original data. So, maximizing the variance of the projected data amounts to maximizing the quadratic form $v^TSv$. We know from the section of Rayleigh quotients that $v^TSv$ is maximized when $v$ is the eigenvector of $S$ that corresponds to the largest eigenvalue of $S$. This is the idea behind principal component analysis.

Principal Component Analysis¶

The goal of PCA is to find an orthogonal $P$ that determines a change of variable such that $X = PY$ (and $Y = P^TX$) with the property that the new variables are uncorrelated. $P$ has dimensions $p\times p$, where $p$ is the number of observations in in column in $X$.

The covariance matrix of the data expressed with new variables $Y$ is $P^TSP$, where $S = \frac{1}{n-1}XX^T$ is the covariance matrix of $X$:

$$\frac{1}{n-1}YY^T = \frac{1}{n-1}(P^TX)(P^TX)^T = \frac{1}{n-1}(P^TX)(X^TP) = P^T\frac{1}{n-1}(XX^T)P = P^TSP$$

This means we want to find a matrix $P$ such that $P^TSP$ is diagonal (all non-diagonal entries are 0, so there is no covariance). The eigenvalue decomposition of $S$ gives us exactly that, because it gives us $S = PDP^T$ (where $D$ is diagonal); if follows that $P^TSP = D$.

Now we know from above that by projecting the data onto the eigenvector that correponds to the largest eigenvalue of the covariance matrix, we maximize the variance of the projected data. In this way, we can use PCA to discard dimensions of the data that contain less variance and thereby reduce the dimensionality of the data while preserving most of the variance. In this case, we can see that the first dimension contains $\frac{90.45}{(90.45+11.91)} = 88.4\%$ of the variance.
